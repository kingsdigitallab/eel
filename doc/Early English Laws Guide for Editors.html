Guidelines for editors of the Early English Laws

In order to obtain a correct output of your edition, it is strongly advised to follow the instructions below in the right order:

Review the metadata of the edition record:

  1. leave the "Abbreviation" and "Version" fields untouched
  2. add a date for your edition in the "Date of edition" field following the suggestions given just below the date field.
  3. add any eventual hyper-archetypes in the bottom section of the page. You will need to use only Greek letters for the hyper-archetypes. Give a brief description for each, as per example below. Remember to "save" or "save and add another" in the bottom right corner of the page whenever you add something.

 
Edit the text of the edition

It is a good idea to familiarise yourself with the editing page before you start adding text to any field. The editing page is made of several components:

  • the text editor itself: in the middle of the page;
  • the text structure: on the left hand side;
  • the commentary editor: under the text editor.
     

It is very important that the next steps are done in the following order:

  1.  
    1. write or paste the plain text of your edition (without commentary, notes or critical apparatus)
    2. mark-up the structure of your text (please see below for more details on how to do this)
    3. mark-up inline elements (additions, deletions, gaps...)
    4. insert page or folio breaks
  2. Add the critical apparatus
  3. Add the commentary
    1. attach commentaries to specific units of your text (more on this below)
    2. format your commentary
    3. mark up references to other texts
    4. mark up people, places, glossary terms and bibliographic references
  4. Remove editorial notes from the text

How do I use the editing page?

The text and commentary editors have toolbars at the top with buttons to mark-up highlighted text.

How do I mark-up the structure of my text?

The text editor offers several options for the marking-up of the text structure:

  • Code (used to wrap a single code or single items in collections of codes, e.g. Quadripartitus)
  • Book
  • Prologue
  • Chapter
  • Division: a subdivision of a chapter or a prologue. For instance Chapter 4.2 would be the second division within a chapter.

It is important is understand that the above structural units form a hierarchy and some combination are obviously inconsistent (e.g. a book within a chapter or a prologue within a division). Although the text editor let you mark up your text in many ways (including possibly inconsistent ways), it is advised to follow a top-down approach: starting with the code, the books then the chapters and prologues and finally with the divisions.

To mark-up a section of your text as any of the above structures:

  1. select the portion of text you are interested in (to select the whole text it is sometimes easier to use the shortcut combination ctrl+A, or cmd+A for mac)
  2. Click on the relevant option to add structure to the text

The structure is summarized in the left hand box, mouse over the box to update the structure display.

How is the text structure numbered?

Sequential numbers are automatically assigned to each structural unit of your text. Those numbers are visible in the text structure panel on the left hand side of your screen. All those numbers are relative to the containing unit. So the second division of any chapter will received the number 2. However the system knows which chapter they belong to and is therefore able to distinguish them when they will be rendered on the website.

As you'll notice, those numbers are not visible in the text editor itself. For this reason you may find it useful to temporarily keep explicitly written numbers within your text (e.g. '[Prol. 1]' for the first prologue or [2.3] for subdivision 2 of chapter 3). At the end of the editing process those numbers should be removed, as they will be redundant with the information already captured by the system.

If the number assigned automatically by the system is incorrect, you can overwrite it by clicking on the corresponding digits in the left hand side panel. A small window will pop up and ask you to correct the number.

If your edition also feature translations or transcriptions, care should be taken to ensure that all texts share precisely the same structure and numbering otherwise the their synchronised rendering on the website will contain discrepancies. The preview page is there to help you detect such problems, you can find a link to it in the top right corner of the editor.

How do I remove the mark up?

The right way to remove the mark up is to first place your cursor anywhere on the marked-up text then click the eraser button ('Clear').

It is better to avoid deleting the mark-up by deleting the text (e.g. with the delete or backspace keys). Doing this could break the structure of your text or leave invisible and polluting mark-up in your text.

How do I save my changes to the database?

Your changes to the text are saved to the database when:

  • You click on the "save" button at the top of the editor. In principle you only need to click this button before leaving the web page (or closing the browser/computer) or at the end of an editing session.
  • You move your mouse over the text structure. That way your text is automatically saved each time you navigate from one portion of your text to another by clicking on the relevant division in the structure.

Inline elements

Inline mark-up refers to the explicit labelling of a sequence of characters or words within a paragraph. This type of mark-up allows you to preserve additional characteristics of your text in a way that is both standard and easily understood and processed by computers. Apart from the 'Gap' mark-up, all the inline elements are edited in the same way: you first highlight the relevant sequence of characters or words with your mouse then you click on the button which correspond to your mark up. With the 'Gap' element you don't need to highlight anything, you just have to place your cursor at the exact place where the gap occurs in the text.

  • Title: the title of a law code, of a book or any other unit of text.
  • Rubric: 'contains the text of any rubric or heading attached to a particular manuscript item, that is, a string of words through which a manuscript signals the beginning of a text division, often with an assertion as to its author and title, which is in some way set off from the text itself, usually in red ink, or by use of different size or type of script, or some other such visual device'.
  • Added: 'contains letters, words, or phrases inserted in the text by an author, scribe, annotator, or corrector'.
  • Deleted: 'contains a letter, word, or passage deleted, marked as deleted, or otherwise indicated as superfluous or spurious in the copy text by an author, scribe, annotator, or corrector'.
  • Gap: 'indicates a point where material has been omitted in a transcription, whether for editorial reasons described in the TEI header, as part of sampling practice, or because the material is illegible, invisible, or inaudible'.
  • Supplied: 'signifies text supplied by the editor for any reason, typically because the original cannot be read because of physical damage or loss to the original'.

To unmark an element, simply place your cursor within it and press the 'clear' button.

People, places, glossary terms and bibliographic references

How do I mark up a person, a place, a glossary term or a reference to a bibliographic entry?

Follow these steps to mark-up a person in your text:

  1. highlight the name of the person in your text.
  2. select the option 'person' in the drop down in the toolbar (at the top of the text editor).
  3. type the name of the person in the little text box on the right of the dropdown and and press ENTER.
  4. if the person is found in the database, it should be included in list under the text box.
    1. If it is not found, no list will appear. It is either because the spelling of the name you have typed does not match the one in the database or simply because the name is not in the database.
    2. Note that you can enter fragments of the entry you are looking for. For instance 'wil' will match 'William I'. This is particularly useful for bibliographic entries as it allows you to search for an entry based on any of its fields (title, year, authors).
  5. click on the relevant entry in that list
  6. the highlighted name in your text should now be marked up in blue

The procedure is the same for places, glossary terms and bibliographic references.

To unmark an element, simply place your cursor within it and press the 'clear' button.

Page and folio breaks

The page break button in the toolbar can be used to specify that the current location of the cursor in your text correspond to the beginning of a new folio side. By specifying the folio break for each witness the website will have enough information to display the correct folio facsimile next to a particular section of your text.

To review or modify the information about a page break, simply place your cursor anywhere within '[PB]' and click the 'Page break' button again. A window will pop up with a form to correct the manuscript and the folio or page number.

To unmark a page break, simply place your cursor within it and press the 'clear' button.

Critical apparatus

How to edit the critical apparatus?

To add the critical apparatus:

  • select the relevant portion of text in the text editor
  • click the Critical Apparatus icon ('A 1 ')
  • a window pops up:
    • the 'lemma' field correspond to the text you have selected
    • each row under that field correspond to different readings:
      • write the alternative reading in the text box (anything surrounded with {} will appear in italic on the website; e.g. 
         {om} locus
        will result into 'om. locus' ).
      • then, on the same row, tick the checkboxes under the witnesses or hyperarchetypes which contain that reading.
    • the rows left empty will be ignored by the system
    • select 'insert' (or cancel to discard the changes)
  • now you should see '[A]' at the end of your selection. This serves as reference to the apparatus you have just created.

To view or edit a critical apparatus:

  • locate the apparatus in your text
  • click on the green [A] in the text and make sure your cursor is blinking within the '[A]'.
  • then click the Critical Apparatus button ('A 1 ')
  • now a window will pop up and display your readings
    • edit the lemma, readings or checkboxes
    • click 'insert' (or cancel to discard the changes)

To delete a critical apparatus:

  • locate the apparatus in your text
  • click on the [A] and make sure your cursor is blinking within the '[A]'.
  • click the eraser button ('Clear').

Commentary

How does the commentary work?

A commentary text box is visible under the main text editor on the editing page. Note that the structure of the commentary closely follows that of the edition. The commentary is broken into smaller parts, each one attached to a particular division of your edition (book, chapter, sub-division, etc.).

To comment on a particular section of your text, simply click on that division in the text structure (on the left hand side on your screen). This will display the corresponding portion of the edition in the main text editor and the corresponding commentary in the box below it where it can be edited.

To remove a commentary, just remove all the commentary text.

The commentary is always saved together with the text of the edition.

Each time your edition is saved, the text structure is refreshed and division of the text containing a commentary will appear there with a small document icon next to them.

How can I explicitly declare a reference to another text?

If your commentary mentions other text which are also part of this project, it is worth marking up those references so they will be clickable on the website.

Here's how to turn the description of a reference into a hyperlink:

  • Highlight the description of your reference with your mouse
  • Click on the button called 'Reference to another text'
  • A new window pops-up to let you enter additional information about the text
    • Type of text let you choose between an edition or a witness and between a transcription or a translation
    • Once the type of text selected, you can choose the actual witness or edition in the 'Text' drop-down
    • If your reference relates to a portion of law code borrowed from another text, you can select 'quotation'. Otherwise set this drop-down to 'other'.
    • The rest of the form let you specify which portion of the text you'd like to reference. This is optional but it is recommended to be as specific as possible as the hyperlink will point exactly to that area.
      • The portion of the text can be defined in one of two ways:
        • it is either a single unit of text (for instance a whole chapter or a sub-chapter). A row will appear at the bottom of the form where you can specify the position of the unit.
        • or it is a range comprised between two units of text (for instance between the second chapter to the first sub-division of the fourth chapter). Two rows will appear at the bottom of the form where you can specify the position of the beginning and the end of the range. Both are inclusive.
      • Note that, depending on the structure of the referenced text and the granularity of the selected units of text, some fields can be left unspecified.
  • Click 'Insert' to confirm the information associated to the reference

To view or modify the information associated with a reference, simply click anywhere in its description (this description is underlined in blue) and then click on the 'Reference' button again. The same window will pop up.

Technical issues

Is the editing interface compatible with my browser?

The editing page contains sophisticated components that require recent browsers to work properly. The supported browser are Firefox 3.6 or later, IE 8 or later.

If you are experiencing problems with Firefox or IE, please report a bug, by sending an email to earlyenglishlaws[at]sas.ac.uk

What is Junicode and how do I install it?

Junicode is one of the rare computer fonts that can display a wide range of medieval characters on a screen.

To install it, first download the file from internet then unzip the file. You should now have four files: Junicode-Bold.ttf, Junicode-BoldItalic.ttf, Junicode-Italic.ttf and Junicode-Regular.ttf.

On Windows, right click each one of those files and select Install in the pop up menu.

On mac, find your Font Book in your computer (you can search for it in the search menu at the top right corner of your screen), then select "Add font" from your "File" menu and add the fonts you just downloaded.
